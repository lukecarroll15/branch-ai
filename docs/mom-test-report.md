BRANCHAI — MOM TEST RESEARCH REPORT
May 2026 | Confidential — Internal Use Only
========================================
OVERVIEW
--------
This report synthesises findings from two parallel Mom Test research surveys conducted in May
2026:
• 8 dyslexic students (ages approximately 15–22)
• 7 special education professionals (SNAs, SENCOs, learning support teachers)
All respondents were approached with behavioural, past-focused questions in line with Mom
Test methodology — no leading questions, no product pitches, no hypotheticals.
Prototype feedback was collected from 6 of the 8 student respondents (all chose Option 1).
========================================
PART 1: STUDENT FINDINGS
========================================
1.1 CORE PAIN POINTS (frequency across 8 respondents)
------------------------------------------------------
• Reading and retention — 8/8
• Concentration and focus — 6/8
• Spelling and writing — 5/8
• Dyslexia impacts life outside school — 5/8
• Knowing where to start studying — 4/8
1.2 WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS STUDENTS STUDY
---------------------------------------
COLOUR AND VISUAL AIDS (mentioned by 6 of 8 students)
"Different colours in the info — if notes had some words in purple, others in orange — helps a
lot."
"I particularly like the words that are highlighted. That is a big help when there is a lot of text."
"I like writing on small coloured notepads — yellow especially. Easier to read for some reason."
AUDIO AND TEXT-TO-SPEECH (mentioned by 4 of 8 students)
"Not having to read. Listening."
"It hurts my head to read."
"The read out loud feature — that is what helps."
QUIZZES AND ACTIVE RECALL (mentioned by 3 of 8 students)

"I like the quiz feature. I find that really helps me to study. Would there be an option for it to
generate another quiz with different questions?"
"Sitting down and trying to remember it" is the core study challenge — the quiz format directly
addresses this.
1.3 AI TOOLS ALREADY IN USE BY STUDENTS
-----------------------------------------
• ChatGPT (most common)
• Claude
• Gemini
• MathGPT
• Study Clix
• Deep AI
• ASMR and audio-first tools
Every student who responded is already using at least one AI tool. The task for BranchAI is not
to introduce students to AI — it is to make AI work better for how they actually learn.
1.4 KEY INSIGHTS — STUDENTS
-----------------------------
COLOUR IS NON-NEGOTIABLE
All students mentioned colour unprompted. Multiple students requested more colour variety —
not just one tint but semantic colour coding (main term vs sub-points vs examples). This is not a
nice-to-have. It is a baseline expectation from the user group.
TTS IS A GAP, NOT A NICE-TO-HAVE
Multiple students are using ASMR, listening apps, and audio-first tools specifically to avoid
having to read. TTS in BranchAI would directly replace these workarounds. Students reaching
for ASMR to study means the product gap is real and present, not hypothetical.
STUDENTS WANT TO KNOW WHERE TO START
"Everything gets left to last minute or all focus on one topic and not the entire module." This
maps directly to the banana split prioritisation feature. It is confirmed user pain, not founder
assumption.
COGNITIVE FATIGUE IS REAL AND DAILY
"It takes extra effort to read everything correctly — very draining." This underpins the entire
product thesis: reduce the effort cost of study, not just the difficulty of content.

2.3 WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS FOR STUDENTS — PROFESSIONAL VIEW
----------------------------------------------------------
• Multi-sensory approaches
• Coloured overlays and highlighting
• Text-to-speech and dictation tools
• Clear step-by-step instructions
• Repetition
• Low student-teacher ratio
• Good student-teacher relationship
• Visual aids and mind maps
2.4 PROCUREMENT — WHO DECIDES
--------------------------------
Procurement ownership is split and often unclear at school level:
• Deputy principal or finance department holds final budget authority (confirmed by Patricia
Frampton, Clongowes)
• SENCO is typically the strongest internal champion — controls paperwork budget, initiates
most SEN tool decisions

• IT group or responsible teacher consulted on technical fit
• SNAs have limited formal influence but strong informal influence through daily use
• AEN consultation team at management level in some schools
2.5 AI ADOPTION AMONG PROFESSIONALS
--------------------------------------
5 of 7 professionals use AI. Tools mentioned: NotebookLM, Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude,
Perplexity, Grok, Copilot, Canva.
Primary use cases: simplifying text, creating accessible materials, rewriting content in easier
forms.
Key gap cited consistently: AI does not replace professional judgement or personal support, and
cannot capture individual student emotional and social context.
2.6 KEY INSIGHTS — PROFESSIONALS
-----------------------------------
RACE ADMIN IS THE BIGGEST TIME SINK
Multiple respondents independently named RACE record-keeping as the most tedious,
time-consuming part of their role. The RACE Evidence Vault feature maps directly to this pain. It
is not a roadmap nice-to-have — it is a confirmed acute pain point with a named, vocal
constituency inside every school.
TEACHER AND LSC BUY-IN IS THE DISTRIBUTION KEY
SNAs and SENCOs are daily users who informally shape procurement long before a deputy
principal signs anything. The product must win with them first. A SENCO who saves hours per
week on RACE records becomes an internal champion — the most valuable sales asset a
school B2B company can have.
CONFIDENCE, NOT JUST CAPABILITY
Professionals emphasise student confidence as the primary non-academic goal across almost
every response. "Do not patronise. Do not dumb down." This principle from Dyslexia Ireland is
confirmed independently by field research. BranchAI's tone and scaffolding design need to
reflect this from the first screen.

PART 3: PROTOTYPE FEEDBACK
========================================
6 students reviewed the current BranchAI prototype. All 6 chose Option 1.
3.1 WHAT STUDENTS LIKED

-------------------------
"I particularly like the words that are highlighted. That is a big help with dyslexia when there is a
lot of text." — Oscar
"Clean and minimal design that makes the content easy to follow. Combining note review with
interactive quiz and AI chat is engaging." — Tom
"I like the quiz feature. I find that really helps me to study." — TU Dublin student
"Very clear and obvious as to what is going on." — Oscar
"It is very good, well done." — Student 6
3.2 WHAT NEEDS IMPROVEMENT
----------------------------
"A little boring. The background colour — the off-shade yellow." Students want stronger visual
interest alongside the cream palette.
"I would suggest adding a second colour in addition to the purple to break up reading. Keep
purple for the main word and use a different colour for A, B, C." — Student 2
"Increase visibility and size of some elements, especially on mobile." — Tom
"Too many words. Looks like any sort of school book." — Student 7
"I am confused by the options — too many words." — Student 8
3.3 FEATURE REQUESTS FROM PROTOTYPE FEEDBACK
----------------------------------------------
• Generate multiple quizzes with different questions — not just one set per topic
• Quick links to YouTube videos for each quiz answer
• More semantic colour variation (not just lavender — add a second distinct colour for
sub-points)
• Mobile sizing — larger tap targets, easier navigation on phone
• Reduce text density in the UI — more visual, less prose-heavy
• Read-aloud and TTS (multiple students, unprompted)
3.4 BOTTOM LINE ON PROTOTYPE
------------------------------
The core concept is validated. Students understand what the product does, respond positively
to it, and want to use it. The two clear problems to solve before the pilot:
1. Reduce text density and increase visual hierarchy — two students said it looks like a school
book. For dyslexic users, that is a serious negative signal.
2. Ship TTS. Multiple students cited audio as their primary coping mechanism for reading-heavy
study. They reached for it unprompted in feedback on a product that does not yet have it.
Both were cited unprompted by multiple students. Neither is a feature request — they are
table-stakes expectations from this user group.

========================================
WHAT THIS RESEARCH CONFIRMS
========================================
1. THE PROBLEM IS REAL

Both cohorts independently confirm the same core pain points — reading load, cognitive
fatigue, and lack of structured study support — without being prompted. This is not founder
assumption.
2. THE PRODUCT DIRECTION IS CORRECT
Colour, TTS, quizzes, and prioritisation support are the top four things students say help them.
BranchAI is built around all four. The research validates the core product thesis.
3. RACE ADMIN IS AN ACUTE PROFESSIONAL PAIN POINT
Named by multiple professionals independently. The RACE Evidence Vault is not a future
feature — it is the fastest path to LSC buy-in and should be treated as such in product
prioritisation.
4. THE PROTOTYPE PASSES THE CONCEPT TEST
6 of 6 students who reviewed it chose Option 1. The feedback is constructive, not dismissive.
The product idea is understood and wanted.
5. TWO PRE-PILOT PRIORITIES ARE NON-NEGOTIABLE
(a) Reduce text density and increase visual hierarchy before any student sees the live product
(b) Ship TTS before any pilot goes live
6. EVERY STUDENT IS ALREADY AN AI USER
The competitive question is not whether students will adopt AI — they already have. The
question is whether BranchAI is more useful than ChatGPT for the specific context of dyslexic
study. That is answerable. The pilot will answer it.
